STH

April 2025

Soil-transmitted helminths (STH) account for the majority of helminth infections and are one of the most prevalent enteric infections world-wide. The current control and elimination strategy for STH is targeted deworming via mass drug administration (MDA) to school-age children and improvements in water, sanitation and hygiene (WASH). Transmission of STH occurs when eggs, that are passed in human feces, contaminate the soil, leading to infections through the ingestion of eggs or via hookworm infective larvae (iL3â€™s) penetrating the skin. Current surveillance strategies for STH focus on identifying and quantifying eggs in stool samples via microscopy, which exhibits poor specificity and sensitivity, especially in settings with low-intensity infections. The limited success of MDA programs may be due, in part, to significant environmental reservoirs capable of re-infecting communities combined with poor resolution surveillance. My postdoctoral research focuses on improving surveillance strategies and methods for STH while gaining insight into drivers of the environmental route of STH infections and monitoring genetic mutations that could lead to drug resistance.

Environmental Surveillance
Wastewater-based epidemiology (WWBE) is an underutilized approach for helminth surveillance. WWBE offers a valuable tool for surveillance of most enteric pathogens but the vast majority of wastewater surveillance studies have focused on bacterial and viral pathogens in high-income countries with networked sanitation systems. Almost 90% of the world's population live in low- and middle-income countries and are often the most vulnerable to STH and extreme weather events. We integrated WWBE with a novel approach for environmental surveillance; isolating environmental DNA (eDNA) from soil collected from locations with high human activity and incorporating sensitive STH species-specific molecular detection assays (qPCR). We evaluated these sampling strategies for soil and wastewater STH surveillance in rural and peri-urban settings without networked sanitation in a cluster randomized control trial (RCT) in two countries (Benin and India). In our pilot study, we detected STH in soil (India = 32/95, Benin = 39/121) and wastewater (India = 24/60, Benin = 8/64) with a detection frequency across all sample types of 36% in India and 25% in Benin. Continued work on this project includes identifying the effects of MDA and climate variations (temperature and precipitation) on STH in the environment. You can read more about this research here.

Drug Resistance
Community-wide mass drug administration (cMDA) has been proposed as a means to more significantly reduce or eliminate STH in some endemic settings compared to standard school-aged based MDA. However, worries of increasing the selective pressure for drug resistance to the benzimidazoles with cMDA have arisen. Communities with cMDA campaigns may impose stronger selective pressures for ABZ resistance in STH compared to school-aged based MDA, supported by a recent predictive model. Reservoirs of infectious stage benzimidazole resistant STH may be found in the environment at higher concentrations with cMDA interventions due to less untreated refugium. While resistance to benzimidazoles is well established in veterinary medicine, there are only limited reports on potential resistance markers in human infective STH. We are uniquely positioned to explore the accuracy of these predictions and determine the abundance of genetic resistance markers using samples from a multi-country RCT. Using STH eDNA collected in our surveillance study and human stool samples collected in the same study area, we will compare STH relative abundance and sequencing depth, accounting for markers of resistance, between treatment arms and across our study sites (MDA vs cMDA, Benin vs India) and determine if eDNA genetic profiles match those of human stool samples. This exciting work is currently underway and I hope to share preliminary results soon!
