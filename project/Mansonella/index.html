Mansonella

Sep 2022

Mansonellosis is an insect-transmitted disease caused by filarial nematodes that infect hundreds of millions of people globally. Despite its prevalence, there are many outstanding questions regarding the general biology of the responsible parasites and their impacts on human health. Historical reports suggest that the Colombian Amazon is co-endemic for Mansonella perstans and Mansonella ozzardi, making it an ideal location to pursue these questions. We have initiated studies to validate diagnostics to measure species-specific mansonellosis prevalence, isolate parasite tissues for genomic analysis and cryopreservation, investigate associations with demographics and other pathogens, and adapt the parasite to the laboratory setting for the study of antiparasitic responses. Across three separate surveys, we have collected blood samples and epidemiological data from >200 adult volunteers in Indigenous communities along the Amazon River and tributaries near Leticia, Colombia. We show that a LAMP assay optimized for detection of the blood-dwelling parasite compares favorably to classical microscopy-based diagnosis. We estimate a disease prevalence of ~40% within these communities and report the successful cryopreservation of these parasites in field settings. Efforts are underway to map Mansonella population genomics, isolate vectors responsible for local transmission, and establish lab infections of Culicoides (biting midges) using cryopreserved microfilariae.
